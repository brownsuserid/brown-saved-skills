# Brain Bridge Email Style Guide

Reference material for drafting sales emails in Aaron's voice. Read this before writing any outreach, follow-up, or sales email.

---

## Aaron Eden: Voice and Tone

Warm, direct, relationship-first. Every email feels like it comes from someone who cares about the recipient's success, not just the transaction.

**Core traits:**
- **Warm and enthusiastic**: Opens with genuine well-wishes. Enthusiasm is authentic, not performative.
- **Relationship-centered**: References shared history, mutual connections, previous conversations.
- **Value-forward**: Leads with something useful (a case study, resource, insight) before requesting time.
- **Optimistic without overselling**: "I'd love to" and "Looking forward to catching up!" create momentum without pressure.
- **Empathetically aware**: Acknowledges the recipient's circumstances before making asks.

---

## Email Structure

Every email follows this architecture:

1. **Greeting + well-wish**: "Hi [Name], I hope you're doing great!"
2. **Contextual bridge**: How you met, who introduced you, what was previously discussed.
3. **Value bridge**: A relevant insight, case study, resource, or acknowledgment.
4. **Core ask/purpose**: Clear statement of intent, usually a meeting request.
5. **Facilitation**: Calendar link with flexible alternatives.
6. **Warm close**: Brief, positive sign-off.

**Sign-off**: Almost always "Best,"

---

## Signature Phrases

| Phrase | Function |
|--------|----------|
| "I hope you're doing great!" | Standard warm opener |
| "I'd love to..." | Requests framed as genuine interest, not obligations |
| "Feel free to grab a spot here:" | Low-pressure calendar invitation |
| "Or if you'd prefer..." | Signals flexibility, respects preferences |
| "Following up on..." | Anchors to previous interaction |
| "Looking forward to catching up!" | Upbeat closer, creates positive expectation |
| "I appreciate..." | Genuine acknowledgment of work, time, efforts |
| "If not, just say the word" | Explicit permission to decline, reduces pressure |
| "Compare notes" | Collaborative framing for initial conversations |
| "Hop on a call" | Casual, low-commitment scheduling language |

---

## Distinguishing Characteristics

- **Calendar link is standard**: Nearly every email includes a Calendly link.
- **Value before ask**: Even in follow-ups, offer something before requesting time.
- **Explicit permission to decline**: "If not, just say the word" builds trust.
- **Relationship continuity**: Consistent references to past conversations.
- **Adaptive formality**: "Yo dude!" with close contacts, "Good morning David" for professional relationships.
- **No dashes**: Use commas, periods, or new sentences instead of em dashes or en dashes.

## Josh Brown: Voice and Tone

Conversational, concise, friendly precision. Blends informal warmth with business clarity. Confident but never pushy.

**Core traits:**
- **Conversational and personable**: "Hey [Name]" or "Hi there," never stiff.
- **Polite and professional**: Courteous without sounding formal.
- **Concise**: Every sentence serves a purpose. Avoids fluff.
- **Emotionally intelligent**: Acknowledges delays or challenges without judgment.
- **Respectful assertiveness**: Sets agendas, makes requests, drives actions without over-explaining.

**Sign-off variations**: "Josh", "Looking forward!", "-Brown"

**Key phrases**: "Just making sure I didn't miss your note", "Wanted to circle back", "No problem at all", "We've got a lot happening"

---

## Rules

- **Never send automatically**. All emails are drafts for Aaron's review.
- **Match sender to context**: Aaron for relationship-driven outreach, Josh for operational/program-related.
- **Default from address**: `aaron@brainbridge.app`
- **Plain text only**: No HTML, no attachments, no tracking pixels.
- **One recipient per email**: No CC/BCC.
